Analogous Colors

Colors that are next to each other on the color wheel, creating a harmonious and visually cohesive combination.

Monochromatic Colors

A color scheme derived from a single base hue and extended using its shades, tints, and tones.

Tetradic Colors

A scheme using four colors that form two complementary pairs, offering rich diversity and balance.

Triadic Colors

A color combination using three colors evenly spaced around the color wheel, offering vibrant contrast.

Complementary Colors

Colors that are directly opposite each other on the color wheel, providing strong contrast when paired.

Warm Colors

Colors like red, orange, and yellow that evoke feelings of warmth, energy, and excitement.

Cool Colors

Colors such as blue, green, and purple that evoke calmness and serenity.

Tints

Tints are lighter versions of a color created by adding white.

Shades

Shades are darker versions created by adding black.

Hue

A term referring to a color or shade in its purest form on the color wheel.

Harmony

Harmony is one of the principles of art that blending well together. Elements that are in sync should exhibit a form of logical progression or connection. It should appear as though the elements are working well with each other.

Balance

Balance refers to the visual weight of elements in a composition. When you look at an art both sides, left to right or top and bottom of the composition feels right, the elements are distributed evenly.

Contrast

Contrast refers to the difference between the elements. When two elements are compared side by side, you can see the noticeable difference or gap between them such as black and white. This can be use to create balance, harmony and emphasis, if either one of elements appear much stronger than the other.

Emphasis

Emphasis refers to the dominance of an element to catch the attention of the viewer's eyes.

Movement

Movement refers to the certain elements that allows you to see movements or lets the viewer's eyes move around the composition. This can be achieve through diagonal or curvy lines and used of illusions.

Pattern

Pattern refers to the repetition of any elements in the composition. This can be seen in tribal fabrics, using geometrical shapes wearvers creates pattern that symbolizes their own culture.

Rhythm

Rhythm refers to the movement created by pattern in a varying yet organized way of elements.

Variety

Variety refers to the varying elements or the use of different elements, adding a sense of chaos in your work. This is the opposite of harmony and unity.

Proportion

Proportion refers to the size of objects in comparison to each other or within a larger whole. When drawing realistic portraits, proportion is very, if one elements doesn't match the subject you are drawing, you would create a whole different person.

Unity

Unity refers to the overall "oneness" of the composition. It tells how the different elements works together.

Scale

Scale refers to the size of work itself or objects in the artwork and how parts relate to each other.

Color Palette

Color palette is a set of colors available or created by the artist that are used to color their own works.

Art

Art is a human activity that includes their creative and imaginative talent which results to a work of art, most of the arts are made to express beauty, emotional power and conceptual idea, though there is no generally agreed definition of what truly art is.

Color theory

It is the study of how humans understand and perceive colors, and how we see the visual effects of how colors mix, match or contrast with one and another. It is also has three categories which has color wheel those 3 are primary, secondary and tertiary.

Harlem Renaissance

The cultural movement that emerged in the 1920s in Harlem, New York City. When a featured African-American artists who rejected stereotypes and celebrated black life and identity through their work in visual arts, literature, music, and theater.

Op art

A type of abstract art that create optical illusions using visual effects. These illusions are often achieved through repetition, patterns, color combinations, and perspective manipulation, providing a sense of depth and movement in the artwork.

Neon art

In the 1960s, to investigate the interplay of light, color, and space, often drawing inspiration from pop culture and consumerism. Neon Art transformed a commonplace commercial 1 neon lighting.

Symbolism

This emphasize emotions and ideas more over objective representation of the natural world. It was an artistic movement plus a literary that has emerged in 19th century, primarily in Catholic European countries. Artists and writers were united by a sense of pessimism and weariness towards modern society's decadence.

Surrealism

An art movement that began in Paris in 1924. The actual goal was explore the irrational and to free the thoughts from the constraints of reason, poetic, and revolutionary.

Dada/Dadaism

Dada is artistic and literary movement in art that has been discovered way back to the First World War, it also been desrcibe to be a way to express artists negative response to the traditional social values and conventional artistics practices of different types of art in their time.

Precisionism

It plays a big role to the rise of American and was the first real indigenous modern art movement in United states. It has some similarities with Cubism and Futurism, Precisionism was created as a way to celebrate the landscape of skycrapers, bridges and factories and also to bring structures back into art.

Post impressionism

Post impressionism simply means the reaction against the naturalistic depiction of light and color of the Impressionists. bold colors and symbolic images has been used by artists such as Van Gogh to used as their way to express their emotional and psychological response to the world.

Pop art

A type of art which has been discovered way back 1950s which is composed by the art of british and american that is inspired from 'popular' imagery and products from commercial. It reached it's peak in the 1960s where people used mechanically reproduced silkscreen, large scale fassimiles, and soft pop art sculpture in their everyday life.

Performance art

Performance Art has been discovered in 1960s which includes different types of art that is created within the use of actions performed by the artist or other participants which can be watched through recordings or live, this type of art challenges the traditional forms of visual art such as painting and sculpture by it's unique style that includes body art, acitons and events.

Futurism

Futurism was an early 20th-century Italian art movement focused on capturing modernity's dynamism and energy, rejecting the past and celebrating industry and technology.

Fauvism

Fauvism, associated with Henri Matisse, was a short-lived style from 1905-1908, characterized by expressive emotionalism and bold color use, focusing on the artist's inner feelings rather than naturalistic representation.

Expressionism

Expressionism is an early 20th-century international trend where artists aimed to express emotional experience rather than physical reality, influencing art, literature, music, theater, and architecture.

Digital art

Digital art is any creative work that uses digital technology as a key part of the artistic process, including mediums like digital photography, computer graphics, pixel art, AI-generated art, and augmented reality art.

Street art

This is a is a type of art that is seen in public on nearby structures, streets, railroads, and other areas that are accessible to the general public. This may be meaningless to most of the individuals but it also represents an idea or message to the public.

Cubism

Cubism is a creative new method of depicting reality that Pablo Picasso and Georges Braque created somewhere between 1907 and 1908. It shows the whole artwork without using such techniques to make it more realistic.

Constructivism

Constructivism is where the individuals actively construct or make their own knowledge and that their experiences as a learner shape reality.

Conceptual art

Also called as conceptualism, is an art where traditional aesthetic, technical, and material concerns are given equal or more weight than the concept or idea that is central to the piece.
