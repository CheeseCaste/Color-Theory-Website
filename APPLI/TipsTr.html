Coloring with Color Theory

As you comprehend color theory beyond all lessons by Hue, dive deeper on the compilation of useful advice and hacks to comprehend and color your artwork with color theory more easily or quickly. #2A: Use of warm colors & cool colors

In the color wheel, warm colors and cool colors are color temperatures that are visible to identify based on how it evokes. It is highly suggested that when creating an artwork such as portrait painting, use warm colors to bring out as cool colors push back. Otherwise, you can make use of warm colors for the main subject of your painting while cool colors for the background just like the picture shown below;

The color red clearly grabs the viewer's attention as it stands out, while the color blue in the backdrop recedes. Both color temperatures are harmonious, with warm colors having more visual weight and creating more sensation.

#2B: Use of warm colors & cool colors

To establish harmony in your artwork, consider using opposite warm and cold colors for lighting and shadows. Simply when your light source is warm, your shadow must be cool; oppositely, when your light source is cool, your shadow must be warm. Look at the photography below;

Direct lighting from this photography, If the light is warmer, the shadow contains less warm light. If the light that strikes an object is cooler, there is less cool light in its shadow. This is what the photo shows; in the first half of the picture, the lighting is cool (green), therefore the shadows are warm (red), similar to the second picture and opposite of the last picture. Use this advice to make the colors in your artwork work together, especially when attempting to symbolize your subject through colors, as shown in the last few sections of this website, specifically "psychology of colors".

#3A: Less color is more or Consider the colors you should use.

Above is a bunch of colors that are difficult to work with. Color your artwork with balance. Too much color with the same vibrancy, tone, and saturation is unpleasant and untidy. Choose an important color and pair it with another hue that promotes harmony. Less color is preferable because it allows you to highlight the subject of your artwork. Use a palette that is compatible with each other and appropriate for the context of your artwork.

#4: Neutral Colors

Neutral colors include black, white, gray, brown, beige, and taupe. Earth tones, or natural colors, are also called neutral. Neutral colors are not visible on the color wheel. Using neutral hues highlights your primary and secondary colors, which helps to complement them. Neutral colors can be used as a base and to fill negative spaces, as demonstrated in the image below.

In the image above, the designer chooses cream or beige neutral hues to keep the interior look tidy. The other colors are well- matched, making it an excellent design for a family room. Neutral colors are essential simply consider what to wear on such occasions when determining which colors complement your skin tone.

#5: Contrast

Contrast is described as the juxtaposition of two distinct parts that work together to form a balanced total. In coloring your artwork, beforehand, try to turn everything into grayscale just like the picture below retrieved from https://i.ytimg.com/vi/LeZc-uYXv3Y/maxresdefault.jpg;

The grayscale image above shows the various values used. Without the contrast of the value, the image or topic will be plain sight, as seen in the photo below.

As a result, value is vital in making artwork to highlight the difference within the subject matter and point out what is significant. No contrast means the color doesn’t matter.

#6: Use 60-30-10 rule

The 60-30-10 rule is a basic design theory that divides a room's colors into three categories: dominant, secondary, and accent. The dominating color should make up 60% of the room's color scheme, followed by the secondary color at 30% and the accent color at 10%. Starting your artwork using this guide can create balance and harmony. Just like the picture shown below;

Understanding the entire color theory is simple; nevertheless, implementing it is tough. These tips and tricks are the most commonly employed by experts, but that doesn't mean you can't set your own guidelines as an artist. Simply recall your aim in your artwork and experience every necessity in your artwork as you begin and continue the progress you've achieved, where you may add any value, saturation, tones, and tints to make your artwork fancy and stunning. The main purpose of coloring with color theory is to achieve harmony and balance throughout your artwork.
