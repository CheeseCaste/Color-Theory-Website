LESSON 1: Color Mixing and Hue

Color Mixing

Mixing two or more colors together and having a different shade of a color as a result is called color mixing. This is a type of color experiment that artists always do when the color that they are going to use isn't available. The color wheel is a visual example of color mixing, and it demonstrates important color relationships (MacNaughton, 2023).

There are 2 mixing models used in different scenarios.

RGB: Additive Color Mixing Model

This is what colors humans see in wavelengths. This uses red, green and blue as the primary colors for mixing. By adding different wavelengths from the visible light spectrum with different levels of intensity, you can create different colors. Adding all different wavelengths, the eyes will see it as white. This model can be used for TVs, phones, tablets or any electronic gadgets since they produce light to display and visualize images. This is why what you print can be different from what you can see on the screen, as the printers use the CMYK color mixing model.

CMYK: Subtractive Color Mixing Model

This model uses colors without the use of wavelength or by subtracting the light by simply adding color. People are more familiar with this method of color mixing since this is what we learn in art class. CMYK stands for cyan, magenta, yellow and key/black, which you can typically see what printers use. It is known previously as your red, yellow and blue colors. However, the creation of printers replaced it with the cyan, magenta, yellow and black. That is why if you ever use the printer, the outcome of the colors is different from when it was on screen. Compared to the RGB model, mixing all colors of the CMYK model results in black.

Hues

Hue is the most basic aspect of color and refers to the specific color itself such as red, blue, yellow, green, and so on. It's the first thing you see when you look at a color, and it's usually determined by its position on the color wheel. The basic colors are primary hues red, blue, and yellow which cannot be created by mixing other colors. These primary colors serve as the building blocks for all other colors. Secondary colors are created when you mix two primary hues together. Tertiary colors are created by mixing a primary color with a secondary color.

Keeping what we learned in color mixing we can create the different hues of the color wheel. Using only the primary colors:
Red + Yellow = Orange.
Yellow + Blue = Green.
Blue + Red = Purple.
The mixtures among the primary colors results in secondary colors.

Using the primary colors with secondary colors:
Red + Orange = Red orange.
Orange + Yellow = Yellow orange.
Yellow + Green = Yellow green.
Green + Blue = Cyan.
Blue + Purple = Indigo.
These colors are known as tertiary colors, a mixture of primary and secondary colors.

The primary, secondary and tertiary colors are what you will see in a typical color wheel. However, it is not only limited to that, there are colors in between the categories.
